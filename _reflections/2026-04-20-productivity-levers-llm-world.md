I distinctly remember the point when I first felt the limit of my personal productivity. Finite resources - time and energy - were exhausted by my current tasks. I had no more juice left to squeeze without giving up sleep or health.

The path forward is well-known: engineers move into leadership. As a manager with 5 engineers, your impact immediately grows to encompass your team's work. As a manager of managers, you can act on and claim strategic initiatives that are nearly impossible to tackle solo.

Yet to do these jobs well, you'll find yourself investing more into soft skills like communication and leadership, and less and less into technical skills like your languages and frameworks. Before long, skills have ossified and your experience is diluted. The unspoken truth is that many engineers are promoted out of IC roles in order to continue growing their careers.

I'll be blunt - this is not a distribution of time that I want. I love code, I tolerate people. Despite this I found myself with only a couple of options:

 1. Bite the bullet, go into leadership. Been there done that.
 2. Stay in IC role, leave growth on the table. Been there done that _also_.

 A bit bleak!

<div class="scene-break">Enter LLMs</div>

A year into the LLM journey, my outlook is quite different:

The range of impact that an IC can produce based on the current generation of tools is _enormous_, and we are still early in a new technology cycle.

Early adopters are already seeing productivity gains equivalent to additional teammates. In practice this looks like 1 lead engineer fully completing a major frontend redesign directly with stakeholders. Or architectural upgrades that used to take weeks going out in a few days, again with just one owner.

I believe there will be both a skill gap between daily users of these tools and late adopters which will feed an employment boon for some. I also believe the tech will continue to improve, so those gains for early adopters will compound.

Much less bleak!
